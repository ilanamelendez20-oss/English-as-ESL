01

How to read a sound

Letters, plus an anchor word

Every sound is written in plain letters and tied to a real example word. Letters alone can’t separate all 44 sounds — a few share a spelling — so the anchor word is the source of truth. The two “th” sounds and the strong vs. weak “uh” are told apart by their label and example, never by spelling alone.

ee as in sheep sh as in ship th as in think · voiceless th as in this · voiced uh as in cup · strong uh as in about · weak
Which English? The clean “20 + 24 = 44” count is the British RP model, so RP is the spine here. General American is rhotic (the “r” sounds after vowels), merges or drops some RP vowels — the “eer/air/oor” gliding vowels mostly don’t exist as separate sounds — and adds r-coloured vowels. Pick one spine and flag the differences rather than mixing them.
02

The vowels · 20

A map of where the tongue goes

The 12 pure vowels live in the space behind your teeth. Left–right is how front or back the tongue bunches; top–bottom is how high or low it sits. “Short” and “long” only describe these 12 — the other 8 are diphthongs that glide. And length matters less than quality: ih ship and ee sheep differ mainly in tongue tension and position, not duration.

The map flattened above is really a side view. Here’s the same handful of sounds as cross-sections — watch the soft body of the tongue rise, fall, and slide back, and the lips spread or round.

a cat

Tongue low & front; the jaw drops open.

Tongue high & back, tense; lips firmly rounded.

uh about · weak

Fully relaxed, mid-mouth — the most frequent sound in English and the key to natural rhythm.

The consonants · 24

Same position, voicing flips

Teach consonants in voiceless / voiced pairs: the mouth holds the same shape, and the only change is whether the vocal folds buzz. There are just two voicing categories — “voiceless” and “unvoiced” are the same thing, never a third option. Put two fingers on your throat: “z” buzzes, “s” doesn’t.

Voiceless — no throat buzz Voiced — folds vibrate

Where each pair is made

04

Spanish-speaker focus · the heart of the app

Five vowels meeting twenty

Spanish runs on 5 vowels (a, e, i, o, u) against English’s ~20, with a simpler syllable shape. That makes the trouble spots few and very teachable — concentrate the drills here.

Vowel confusions

ih vs ee

One Spanish “ee” has to split in two. The #1 vowel confusion.

ship / sheep · bit / beat · live / leave

a vs eh vs uh

Three English sounds collapsing toward one Spanish vowel.

bat / bet / but · cat / cut · bad / bed

uu vs oo

Short relaxed vs. long tense back rounding.

full / fool · pull / pool

the weak uh & weak forms

Spanish gives every vowel its full value, so unstressed syllables don’t reduce. The single biggest driver of a non-native rhythm.

banana · to · of · a · for → “uh”

Consonant confusions

b vs v

In Spanish these are one sound — a very persistent merger.

berry / very · bat / vat · boat / vote

the two th sounds

Castilian already has the voiceless “th” (cielo, zapato); Latin American Spanish doesn’t, so speakers often swap in “t,” “s,” or “d.”

think · this

s vs z

Spanish has no “z” — this hits every -s plural and verb ending.

Sue / zoo · place / plays

sh vs ch

Many dialects lack “sh,” so it becomes “ch.”

sheep / cheap · share / chair

j · y · zh

Easily blurred together.

jet / yet · jello / yellow · measure

h

Silent in Spanish, so it may drop; meanwhile the throaty Spanish “j” gets swapped in for English’s light “h.”

house · here · behind

ng

Tendency to add a hard “g” (“sing-guh”) or swap in “n.”

sing · singer

r

The Spanish tapped/trilled r vs. the English gliding r — one of the most audible markers.

red · car · around

dark l

English makes the end-of-syllable “l” heavy and dark; Spanish “l” stays light and clear.

milk · full · well

Signature pattern · syllable structure

The “eh” before s-clusters

Spanish words don’t begin with s + consonant, so an “eh” gets added in front. The clearest Spanish-English fingerprint:

school → eschool · Spain → Espain · student → estudent · special → especial

Two more cluster habits to drill: final consonants dropped or de-voiced (dog → “dok,” big → “bik,” and → “an”), since Spanish syllables usually end in a vowel; and cluster simplification in words like texts, asked, sixths, worlds.

Rhythm beats any single sound English is stress-timed — it squeezes unstressed syllables, which is why the weak “uh” exists. Spanish is syllable-timed, giving each syllable near-equal time. Teaching rhythm, sentence stress, and weak forms does more for intelligibility than perfecting any one sound.

Building it

App structure & resources

Suggested screens

  1. The Sound Map — all 44 sounds; tap one for audio + articulation animation.
  2. Vowels — short / long / diphthongs, each with spelling, example words, audio, mouth animation, minimal-pair drill.
  3. Consonants — the 8 voiceless/voiced pairs, the sonorants, and “h”.
  4. Spanish-Speaker Track — drills for the confusions above, plus dedicated s-cluster and final-consonant modules.
  5. Listen & Discriminate — A/B minimal pairs (“ship or sheep?”) before production.
  6. Produce & Compare — record the learner, play back beside the native model.
  7. Rhythm & Connected Speech — the weak “uh,” weak forms, sentence stress.

An “L1 = Spanish” toggle surfaces the contrastive tips and reorders drills by difficulty; track progress per sound; offer both isolated-sound and in-word/in-sentence views.
